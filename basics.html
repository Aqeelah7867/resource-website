Safety

While embroidery is a relaxing and enjoyable craft, it’s important to practice proper safety measures to prevent injuries and ensure a smooth stitching experience. Handling sharp tools like needles, scissors, and embroidery machines requires care and attention.

Handle Needles with Care

  • Always hold the needle by the blunt end to prevent accidental pricks.
  • Avoid rushing or stitching too quickly to maintain control
  • When stitching, pay attention to where your non-stitching hand is to avoid poking yourself. Consider using a thimble if you frequently prick your fingers while pushing the needle through fabric.

    • Use a Needle That Matches Your Fabric

  • A needle that’s too thick for the fabric can cause damage, while a very thin needle may bend or break.
  • Choose the right needle type for your embroidery project to make stitching easier and safer.

    • Needles in a Safe Place

  • Store needles in a pincushion, needle case, or magnetic holder when not in use.
  • Never leave loose needles on your workspace, clothing, or furniture where they can get lost and cause injury.

    • Needles

    Crewel Needles

    The most commonly used hand embroidery needle. Has a sharp point and a long, oval eye for threading embroidery floss. Comes in various sizes (higher numbers mean thinner needles).

  • Best for: General embroidery with cotton or silk floss.
  • Features: Sharp point, long, oval eye for easy threading.

    • Chenille

    Similar to crewel needles but with a larger eye and a sharp point. Perfect for embroidery on thicker fabrics and for techniques like ribbon embroidery.

  • Best for: Embroidery with thick threads, ribbon embroidery.
  • Features: Sharp point, large eye.
  • Why? The sharp tip allows for easy penetration of heavier fabrics, while the large eye accommodates thicker threads or ribbon.

    • Embroiderery Floss

    Understanding Floss Strands

    Embroidery floss is typically made up of 6 individual strands of thread twisted together. fine lines or very detailed stitches, you can use just one strand of embroidery floss, For thicker, more solid stitches, you can use multiple strands. Typically, 2-3 strands are used

    Types of fabrics

    Cotton

    Best for: Beginners, general embroidery, and cross-stitch.

    is lightweight, breathable, and easy to stitch on. Tightly woven options being ideal for embroidery.

    Linen

    Best for: Hand embroidery, counted-thread embroidery, and heirloom projects.

    Linen has a natural texture and durability that makes it great for detailed embroidery.

    EvenWeave

    Best for: Detailed embroidery, cross-stitch, and blackwork.

    Evenweave fabrics have a consistent thread count, making them perfect for detailed stitching.
